How to use this framework

This framework is a tool for reflection, growth, and self-assessment. It centers on two questions:

  • What capabilities do you possess? (Y-axis)
  • How do you engage and apply those capabilities? (X-axis)

It helps learning scientists examine not just the capabilities they hold, but how they put them into practice. The Y-axis—Context, Foresight, and Expertise—represents the depth of capacity required to understand learning environments, anticipate future opportunities and challenges, and contribute specialized knowledge. The X-axis—Human, Collaborative, Boundary Spanning, Expert Generalist, Connectivist, and Adaptive—represents the stances through which that capacity is expressed. Together, these dimensions provide a holistic view of professional growth, helping individuals identify strengths, surface developmental opportunities, and chart a path from novice practitioner toward increasingly sophisticated, impactful learning science practice. In an era shaped by AI, the framework also encourages practitioners to reflect on which capabilities can be augmented by technology and which human-centered stances remain essential for making sense of complex learning systems and creating meaningful change.

Professional Capacity (Y) —
Context
Understands the broader organizational, industry, and field context in which work occurs. Connects current priorities to historical lessons, emerging trends, research, and external developments. Leverages knowledge of what has been tried before—both successes and failures—to inform decisions and identify opportunities. Uses evidence, benchmarking, and credible sources to deepen understanding, challenge assumptions, and help others see the bigger picture, while maintaining the awareness to situate that evidence within organizational, cultural, and systemic realities.
Foresight
Anticipates needs, opportunities, risks, and challenges before they become apparent to others. Draws on deep expertise, experience, and pattern recognition to identify potential obstacles, dependencies, and unintended consequences early in the process. Challenges assumptions and asks thoughtful questions that clarify scope, surface hidden risks, and expand thinking. Looks beyond the immediate task or project, considering both short- and long-term implications of decisions and actions. Envisions desired future outcomes and works backward to identify the strategies, milestones, and resources needed to achieve them. Proactively prepares for what is likely to come next, helping others navigate complexity, avoid preventable problems, and successfully deliver results.
Expertise
Demonstrates deep knowledge and proficiency in a specialized area of practice. Brings a strong command of the methods, frameworks, theories, tools, and/or technical skills that underpin high-quality work. Maintains and continuously develops professional expertise to stay current with evolving practices, technologies, and standards. Applies specialized knowledge to solve complex problems, produce rigorous work, and provide credible guidance to others. Expertise is the individual’s unique contribution—their “color in the crayon box”—that enables them to create value and advance the work with confidence and precision.

Adaptive Stance (X) —
The orientation through which an individual engages with their work, learning, and changing conditions. Demonstrates curiosity, openness, and a willingness to evolve thinking as new information emerges. Seeks feedback, tests assumptions, and adapts approaches based on evidence and experience. Remains effective in ambiguity and complexity, balancing confidence in expertise with humility and a commitment to continuous learning. An adaptive stance enables individuals to deepen their context, strengthen their expertise, and sharpen their foresight over time.
Human (people-centered)
Centers the experiences, needs, and perspectives of learners and stakeholders. Approaches work with empathy, curiosity, and respect for diverse lived experiences. Considers the human impact of decisions and bridges data, evidence, and human insight.
Collaborative (relationship-oriented)
Builds and sustains reciprocal relationships that advance learning and collective success. Actively seeks diverse perspectives, works effectively with others, and contributes to environments of trust, shared ownership, and mutual accountability.
Boundary Spanning (integrative)
Works fluidly across disciplines, teams, functions, and organizational levels. Connects ideas, people, and perspectives that might otherwise remain separate and translates effectively between different domains of expertise and practice.
Expert Generalist (breadth/depth-oriented)
Demonstrates deep expertise in at least one area while maintaining working knowledge across adjacent domains. Integrates multiple perspectives, methods, and forms of expertise to address complex problems and identify opportunities that may not be visible within a single discipline.
Connectivist (networked view of knowledge)
Views knowledge as distributed across people, communities, technologies, and information networks. Actively learns from and contributes to communities of practice, recognizing that expertise resides not only within individuals but also within relationships, tools, and collective intelligence.
Adaptive (learning-oriented)
Responds effectively to change, ambiguity, and emerging information. Tests assumptions, incorporates feedback, and adjusts approaches based on evidence and experience. Remains resilient, iterative, and committed to continuous learning and improvement.
